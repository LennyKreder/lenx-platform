My love triangle between analog vs digital

I have a confession. I sell digital planners, and I'm also completely obsessed with paper.

I have more Filofaxes than I'm willing to admit. Different sizes, different setups, and I rotate between them depending on the mood and the moment. I fill them with papers I print myself. I own a Lamy Scala Piano Black, a Lamy Dialog CC, and a Pilot Decimo. If you know those names, you know the problem. If you don't — they're fountain pens that cost more than some people's phones. And I regret nothing.

There's a feeling you get when you write with a really good pen on really good paper that no stylus in the world can replicate. The weight of the pen, the scratch of the nib, the way the ink flows. It's almost meditative. I could write grocery lists with my Dialog CC and it would feel like art.

So how do I also make digital planners? Isn't that contradictory?

I don't think so. I think they solve different problems. My Filofax is my slow space. It's where I sit down, take a breath, and write without a screen glowing at me. There's intention in pulling out a fountain pen. You don't rush with a fountain pen. You can't. The ink won't let you.

My iPad planner is my fast space. It's where I manage deadlines, check off tasks, and navigate between months in two taps. It's efficient. It's always synced, always with me, always organized. When I need to find something from three weeks ago, I don't flip — I tap.

The truth is, I go back and forth. Some weeks I'm fully digital. Everything on the iPad, everything linked, everything perfect. Other weeks I crave the analog ritual. The sound of a pen on paper. The tactile satisfaction of flipping a real page. The Filofax comes out and the iPad goes in the drawer.

And then I print new inserts for the Filofax. With my own designs. Because of course I do. I can't just use any insert — it has to be the right layout, the right spacing, the right weight of lines. This is exactly the same obsession that led me to build the digital planners. I'm solving the same problem in two different formats.

I think there's a myth that you have to choose. Paper or digital. Analog or modern. Fountain pen or Apple Pencil. But why? I use my Pilot Decimo at my desk in the morning. I use my iPad on the couch at night. Different tools, different moments, same brain that needs to get things out of my head and onto something.

Each format has its strengths that the other simply can't match. Paper doesn't run out of battery. It doesn't get software updates that move your buttons around. It doesn't notify you about anything. It just sits there, waiting, patient. There's a reliability to paper that technology hasn't figured out yet.

But digital doesn't run out of pages. It doesn't smudge. It lets me undo. It gives me infinite colors, clickable navigation, and the ability to duplicate a layout I love across an entire year in seconds. Try doing that with a hole punch and a printer. Actually, I have tried that. It takes forever - but is deeply satisfying.

The fountain pen thing is its own rabbit hole, honestly. Each pen writes differently. The Scala is precise and elegant — perfect for small, neat handwriting. The Dialog CC has that satisfying click mechanism and writes like butter. The Decimo is compact enough to carry everywhere, which is dangerous because it means I always have a reason to write something down. They each have their personality, their mood, their moment. I'll write about each of them properly at some point. They deserve their own stories.

And my Filofax — don't even get me started. The rings, the dividers, the act of punching holes in a freshly printed insert and clicking it into place. There's a whole community of Filofax nerds out there and I am proudly one of them. We trade inserts, we customize setups, we debate ring sizes. It's a thing. A beautiful, slightly ridiculous thing.

If you're a paper person, I get you. I am you. And I'll probably make printable Filofax inserts at some point because I can't help myself. If you're a digital person, that's what the iPad planners are for. And if you're like me — stuck in an eternal love triangle between the two — welcome. There's no winning. There's only buying more fountain pens and downloading more planners.
