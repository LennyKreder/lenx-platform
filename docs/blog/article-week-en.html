How I Organize My Week

February 2026

People always ask me what my planning routine looks like. Which is funny, because it took me a long time to even have one. I used to just wing it. Monday morning, stare at the ceiling, figure out what needs to happen. That worked until it didn't.

The turning point was a week where I forgot a deadline, double-booked myself, and realized at 4pm on Friday that I hadn't done the one thing I actually needed to do all week. Everything else? Done. The important thing? Completely forgotten. That's when I decided I needed a system. Not a complicated one. Just something.

Now I do a weekly review on Sunday evening. It takes maybe fifteen minutes. I open my planner, look at last week, and move anything unfinished to the new week. Not everything gets moved. Some things looked important on Monday but by Friday they clearly weren't. Those get deleted. Guilt-free.

This is my favorite part, actually. The deleting. It's like cleaning out a closet. You realize half the things you were carrying around didn't need to be there. That task you added on Wednesday? It was urgent in the moment but now it's irrelevant. Gone. The mental space that frees up is real.

Then I look at the week ahead. I don't plan every hour. That stresses me out and I'll ignore it anyway. I just write down the big things — deadlines, appointments, things I'll regret not doing. The rest fills itself in. I've tried time-blocking. Color-coding. Hourly schedules. They all lasted about four days before I abandoned them. What works for me is a loose structure with room to breathe.

I usually have three to five things per day. If I write down more than five, I already know I'm lying to myself. I won't do all of them. And then I feel bad about it. Three things that actually get done is better than eight things that make me feel like a failure. Less is more, especially with to-do lists.

There's a trick I've learned over time, and it's embarrassingly simple: I put the thing I'm most likely to avoid first. Not the biggest task. Not the most urgent. The one I keep pushing to tomorrow. Because if I get that one done before lunch, the rest of the day feels like a victory lap. And if I don't put it first, it just migrates from day to day until it either becomes a crisis or becomes irrelevant. Neither is ideal.

During the week, I use the daily pages. Not as a strict schedule, but as a place to dump whatever is in my head. Thoughts, tasks, reminders, ideas. Sometimes it's a proper to-do list. Sometimes it's just "call the dentist" and nothing else. Both are fine.

I've stopped judging my daily pages. They used to stress me out if they weren't neat or complete. Now I treat them as a workspace, not a presentation. Some days are messy. Some days have one line. Some days are covered in arrows and cross-outs because plans changed three times before noon. That's not a failure. That's life. The planner absorbs the chaos so my brain doesn't have to.

I also use the notes pages more than I expected. Random ideas, sketches for new planner layouts, lists of things I want to research. They don't belong in any specific day, but they need to live somewhere. Having blank note pages inside my planner means I don't need a separate notebook. Everything is in one place.

This is something I designed into the planners intentionally. Because I kept running into it myself — an idea at 10pm that doesn't belong on tomorrow's to-do list but I'll forget it if I don't write it down now. Notes pages catch those. No category, no date, no pressure. Just a place to put things.

The habit tracker is the one thing I didn't think I'd use. I thought it was one of those Instagram productivity things that looks good in photos but doesn't actually help. Turns out, seeing a row of completed checkmarks does something to my brain. Not in a hustle-culture way. More like, oh, I actually did drink enough water this week. Good for me.

I track simple things. Water, exercise, reading, whether I journaled that day. Nothing ambitious. The point isn't to optimize my life. The point is to notice patterns. Like, I always skip exercise on Wednesdays. Why? No idea. But now I know, and I can do something about it. Or not. At least I'm aware.

The monthly overview is where I do my big-picture thinking. Birthdays, deadlines, trips, launches. I don't write much on it — just enough to see what's coming. It's like looking at a map before you start driving. You don't need every street, just the major turns. My monthly view gives me that. One glance and I know if the week ahead is calm or chaotic.

The thing about a planning routine is that it has to fit your life, not the other way around. If your system requires an hour every Sunday and a color-coded legend, you'll drop it within a month. Mine takes fifteen minutes and a cup of tea. That's sustainable. That's the kind of system you'll still be using in December.
