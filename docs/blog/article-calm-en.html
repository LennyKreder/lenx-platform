Designing for Calm

February 2026

There's a moment when I'm designing a new planner page where I have to stop myself. Because I always want to add one more thing. One more section. One more tracker. One more line.

And then I remember — the whole point is space. White space. Breathing room. A page that doesn't ask too much of you.

Most planners I see online are impressive. They have color-coded sections, icons, motivational quotes, sticker areas. They're beautiful. And they overwhelm me within seconds. Where do I even start? What goes where? Why is there a section for my moon phase?

I get the appeal. A full page feels like you're getting your money's worth. But I've found the opposite to be true. The more a page offers, the less I actually use it. I skip sections, I leave boxes empty, and then I feel like I'm doing it wrong. A planner should never make you feel like you're doing it wrong.

My approach is the opposite. Every element on the page has to earn its place. If I can't explain why it's there in one sentence, it goes. A grid for writing? Yes. A time column? Yes. An inspirational quote about being your best self? No. You don't need your planner to motivate you. You need it to get out of your way.

This philosophy comes from my work as a developer, actually. In code, the best solution is usually the simplest one. Less code means fewer bugs, easier maintenance, and better performance. Planners work the same way. Less clutter means fewer decisions, easier navigation, and a better experience. The principles transfer surprisingly well.

The hardest part of designing a minimalist planner isn't adding things. It's resisting the urge to add things. Every blank space feels like a missed opportunity. But blank space IS the feature. That's where your thoughts go. That's where your handwriting lives. If I fill every corner with pre-made sections, where do you put the things I didn't think of?

I had a version once where I added a gratitude section to the daily page. Three lines, write down what you're grateful for. It's a nice idea. Lots of planners have it. But when I tested it myself, I skipped it every single day. Not because I'm not grateful, but because it felt forced. Like homework. So I removed it. If you want a gratitude practice, you can write it in the open space. But I'm not going to tell you to do it.

That's the key difference, I think. Prescriptive versus permissive. A prescriptive planner tells you how to plan: here's your section for goals, here's your meal plan, here's your water tracker, here's your mood chart. A permissive planner gives you space and lets you decide. Some days you need a to-do list. Some days you need to brain-dump. Some days you just need a blank page. My planner allows all three.

I test every layout myself before I release it. I actually use it for a week. If something feels off — if I keep skipping a section, or if I wish I had more room somewhere — I change it. The planners aren't designed in theory. They're designed by someone who actually plans with them. Every day. Every week. If a page annoys me on day three, it's not going out.

Some of the best changes I've made came from small frustrations. The time column was originally too narrow. I kept writing outside the lines. So I made it wider. The to-do checkboxes were too small for the Apple Pencil. So I made them bigger. These aren't revolutionary design decisions. They're just the result of actually using what you build. More people should try that.

There's a concept in design called "reducing cognitive load." Fancy words for a simple idea: don't make people think more than they need to. Every extra element on a page is a decision. Should I fill this in? What goes here? Did I miss something? Multiply that by 365 days and you've added hundreds of tiny decisions to someone's year. I'd rather give you a clean page and let you decide what it needs today.

I also think about the feeling when you open the planner. That first second. If the page is busy, the first feeling is overwhelm. Your brain has to process everything before it can start. If the page is clean, the first feeling is calm. You breathe. You think. You write. That one-second difference matters more than people realize.

At the end of the day, a planner is a tool. And a good tool disappears into the background while you focus on the work. You don't think about a good hammer. You just hammer. I want my planner to feel the same way. You open it, you plan, you close it. No friction, no confusion, no noise. Just calm.
